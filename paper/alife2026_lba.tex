\begin{abstract}
Cellular automata (CAs) often generate extended periodic domains together with
localized structures and interfaces between domains. We present \emph{Winnower}, a
model-selection method for inferring a periodic spatiotemporal domain from a
finite CA space--time observation. A candidate temporal period and spatial
displacement induce translation orbits over the observed sites. Assigning each
orbit its majority state gives the domain template with minimum Hamming
disagreement for that candidate. Winnower ranks candidates using the Bernoulli normalized maximum
likelihood (NML) criterion and returns the selected template and a defect
mask. In preliminary one-, two-, and
three-dimensional experiments, the selected symmetry stabilized after short
transients. The NML complexity term also prevented systematic period
inflation: for 105 nontrivial Life-like rules, unpenalized likelihood selected
the largest scanned period in every case, whereas NML selected period~1 for 97
rules. Several defect masks retained persistent localized structure. These
results suggest that Winnower can provide a global preprocessing step for CA
rule-space surveys before local particle or coherent-structure analysis. Data
and code: \url{https://github.com/zitterbewegung/Winnower}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A cellular automaton (CA) updates a regular lattice synchronously under a local
transition rule. Many rules produce space--time diagrams with extended periodic domains,
localized structures such as particles or gliders, and interfaces between
domains. The domain provides the background against which such
structures are defined. We ask which temporal period and spatial displacement
give the simplest adequate domain model for a finite observation.

Local causal-state methods reconstruct local spatiotemporal organization
\citep{rupe2024}, while taxonomies, transient analyses, and compression-based
approaches characterize rule-level dynamics
\citep{vispoel2022,rollier2024,hudcova2021,alfaro2024,cisneros2023}. Winnower
complements these approaches by fitting global periodic domain templates with
a complexity penalty suitable for broad rule-space sweeps.

\section{Method}
Given a finite binary space--time block $U$, temporal period $p$, spatial
displacement vector $\bs$, and periodic lattice extents $\bD$, Winnower fits
templates satisfying
$B[t{+}p,\,\mathbf{x}{+}\bs \bmod \bD]=B[t,\mathbf{x}]$. The translation
partitions observed sites into orbits $\{O_j\}$. Assigning each orbit its
majority state yields the template with minimum Hamming disagreement for the
fixed candidate; mismatches form the defect mask
(\cref{fig:winnower-pipeline}). Candidates are ranked by the Bernoulli NML
criterion
%
\begin{equation}
\NML(p,\bs)=\sum_j n_j\,\Hb(\hat\theta_j)\;+\;\sum_j \mathcal{C}(n_j),
\label{eq:nml}
\end{equation}
%
where $n_j=|O_j|$, $\hat\theta_j=n_j^{(1)}/n_j$, $\Hb$ is binary entropy, and
$\mathcal{C}(n)=\log_2\!\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}(k/n)^{k}((n{-}k)/n)^{\,n-k}$
is the exact Bernoulli NML (Shtarkov) complexity of an $n$-observation class.
The first term is the orbitwise Bernoulli NLL at the maximum-likelihood
estimates; the implementation evaluates $\mathcal{C}$ exactly for small orbit
classes and by its leading asymptote $\tfrac12\log_2(n\pi/2)$ for larger ones
\citep{shtarkov1987,grunwald2007,yamanishi2023}.

The penalty is needed because a compatible longer-period candidate can refine
the orbit partition of a shorter candidate with the same drift. Refinement
cannot worsen Hamming disagreement or unpenalized likelihood, so fit-only
selection systematically favors longer periods. NML penalizes the additional
orbit models. We formalized the corresponding divisibility and
orbit-refinement result in Lean~4 \citep{achim2025}. Once candidates differ in
per-site NLL rate, the linear fit gap dominates the logarithmic penalty gap,
giving stable selection as the horizon grows.


\section{Preliminary Results}
We evaluated Winnower on elementary CA Rules~30, 54, and 110;
two-dimensional Life-like and totalistic rules; and three-dimensional
Life-like rule B5/S45 \citep{bays1987,bays2006}. \Cref{fig:results} pairs the
decomposition and horizon sweep for the two elementary rules ECA-54 and
ECA-110; 2D and 3D runs use seed~11 (1D/2D) and seed~42 ($T\le50$, 3D). Rule~54
selected period~4 throughout, with its margin increasing from 681 to 2318
bits. B5/S45 selected period~1 throughout, with its margin increasing from
3324 to 7403 bits. Rule~110 changed from period~1 to period~7, consistent with
its periodic ether background, and S24/B11 changed from period~1 to period~2.
Their margins increased after the transition.

At $T{=}100$ in a sweep of 105 nontrivial Life-like rules, unpenalized NLL
selected the largest scanned period for every rule, whereas NML selected
period~1 for 97. Entropy-rate comparisons indicate that the inferred template
captures a predictable component of the observation while the defect mask retains
structured variation. In rules such as S37/B11, the defect population
persisted and increased with lattice size \citep{secktuohmora2023}. These
defects are candidate departures from the domain, not identified particles.

\texttt{analyze3d} reproduce individual runs; \cref{fig:results} is generated

\section{Discussion and Future Work}
Winnower returns a global periodic domain template and defect mask. It assumes
binary states, periodic boundaries, a supplied candidate set, and one global
symmetry, and so complements rather than replaces local causal-state or
particle analysis \citep{rupe2024,rupe2025}.
Future work will test sensitivity to observation length, boundaries, noise,
and candidate-set size; connect defect masks to local tracking; and extend the
method to multistate, continuous-state, and neural cellular automata
\citep{hamon2024,plantec2025,niklasson2021}.
