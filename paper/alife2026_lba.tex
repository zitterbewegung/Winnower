\begin{abstract}
Cellular automata often produce space-time patterns comprising broad periodic
domains together with defects, particles, and interfaces. We introduce
\emph{Winnower}, a software tool and model-selection framework for detecting
these domains from finite observations. Each candidate period/shift pair
induces a relative-periodic orbit partition, is fitted to its nearest binary
background by an orbit-wise majority vote, and scored against the other
candidates by the Bernoulli normalized maximum likelihood (NML) criterion. The
outputs are a selected periodic background and a residual mask that isolates
structured departures from it. Across one, two, and three dimensions selected
periods lock in after short transients, the complexity penalty prevents
systematic period inflation, and residual masks flag rules with persistent
structured defects. Winnower is a global front end for artificial-life
analysis: it proposes a compact domain hypothesis fast enough for large rule
sweeps, leaving the residual for deeper particle or coherent-structure work.
Data and code: \url{https://github.com/zitterbewegung/Winnower}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Cellular automata (CA) generate space-time patterns in which large regular
domains coexist with defects, particles, and interfaces. In artificial life
these domains matter because they are the substrate on which information
transmission, coherent structures, and emergent computation become visible. A practical question follows: given a
finite CA space-time, what is the simplest global periodic organization that
best explains it, and what structured residual remains once it is removed?

Existing approaches optimize different objectives. Computational mechanics and
local causal-state methods reconstruct local structure in detail
\citep{rupe2024}, while survey-
and compression-based traditions classify rules globally and by scale
\citep{vispoel2022,rollier2024,hudcova2021,alfaro2024,cisneros2023}.
Winnower aims at a middle ground: a global, complexity-penalized selector for
periodic templates, fast enough for wide scans.

\section{The Winnower Algorithm}
Given a binary spacetime $U$, a shift $\bs$, and a period $p$, Winnower fits
backgrounds satisfying $B[t{+}p,\,\mathbf{x}{+}\bs \bmod \bD]=B[t,\mathbf{x}]$.
The corresponding space-time translation partitions the observations into orbit
classes $\{O_j\}$; each class is given its majority symbol, so fitting is a
collection of independent binary votes and costs one linear pass per candidate.
Candidates are ranked by the Bernoulli NML score
%
\begin{equation}
\NML(p,\bs)=\sum_j n_j\,\Hb(\hat\theta_j)\;+\;\tfrac12\sum_j \log_2 n_j ,
\label{eq:nml}
\end{equation}
%
with $n_j=|O_j|$, $\hat\theta_j=n_j^{(1)}/n_j$, and $\Hb$ the binary entropy:
a Bernoulli negative log-likelihood (NLL) fit term plus the asymptotic NML
complexity of independent Bernoulli classes \citep{shtarkov1987,grunwald2007,yamanishi2023}.

The penalty is essential. When a longer period is a velocity-matched multiple
of a shorter one, its orbit partition \emph{refines} the shorter one, so it can
only lower the residual and the NLL --- never raise them. Fit-alone selection
therefore inflates the period along every such chain, whether or not extra
structure is present; the second term of \cref{eq:nml} neutralizes exactly that
pressure. This boundary (velocity-matched divisibility is precisely when one
template universally refines another) is the penalty's rationale, and its
structural core is machine-checked in Lean~4 under public CI \citep{achim2025}.
Once candidates reach distinct per-site NLL rates, the linear-in-horizon fit
gap dominates the logarithmic penalty, so the lowest-rate candidate is selected
and the choice locks; after a finite transient the smallest true period is
recovered.

\section{Experiments}
Winnower was evaluated on 1D elementary CA (rules 30, 54, 110), broad Life-like
and totalistic 2D surveys, and a 3D totalistic rule \citep{bays1987,bays2006}.
Selected periods either lock immediately (rule~54 at period~4; the 3D rule at
period~1) or pass through short transients before settling (rules~110 and
S24/B11), and in every case the winning margin grows after the final
transition; every tested rule made at most two transitions before locking. The
complexity penalty is what makes this work: scoring the same families by raw
residual, by NLL, and by NML, only NML avoids period inflation
(\cref{fig:ablation}) --- plain NLL picks the largest period for all 105
rules. Finally, the residual is not noise: an entropy-rate comparison shows the
background captures the predictable component while the residual retains
structured uncertainty, and 2D rules such as S37/B11 leave residual
populations that persist and scale with grid size --- the localized activity
that glider and coherent-structure detectors target
\citep{secktuohmora2023}.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
Winnower turns periodic-domain selection into a concrete model-selection
problem: orbit classes make background fitting fast, and the NML score explains
both why unpenalized selectors overfit and why the penalized selector locks. It
does not replace local causal-state reconstruction
\citep{rupe2024,rupe2025} and makes no claim that the selected period
is always ``true''; it offers a compact domain hypothesis, a residual mask, and
a principled comparison across large rule families, and the same front-end role
extends to modern continuous and learned substrates
\citep{hamon2024,plantec2025,niklasson2021}. In this sense it is a scalable
global front end for the study of emergent space-time organization, not a full
account of CA dynamics.
