\begin{abstract}
Cellular automata often produce space-time patterns comprising broad periodic
domains, together with defects, particles, and interfaces. We introduce
\emph{Winnower}, a software tool and a model-selection framework for detecting
these domains from finite observations. Each candidate period/shift pair
induces a relative-periodic orbit partition, is fitted to its nearest binary
background by an orbit-wise majority vote, and is scored against the other
candidates using the Bernoulli normalized maximum likelihood (NML) criterion.
The resulting outputs are a selected periodic background and a residual mask
that isolates structured departures from that background. Experiments in one,
two, and three dimensions are consistent with this picture: selected periods
lock in after short transients, the complexity penalty prevents systematic
period inflation, and residual masks identify rules with persistent structured
defects. Winnower is intended as a global front end for artificial-life
analysis; it proposes a compact domain hypothesis rapidly enough for large
rule sweeps, leaving the residual for deeper particle or coherent-structure
analysis. Data and code: \url{https://github.com/zitterbewegung/Winnower}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Cellular automata (CA) frequently generate space-time patterns in which large
regular domains coexist with defects, particles, and interfaces. In artificial
life these domains matter because they often provide the substrate on which
information transmission, coherent structures, and emergent computation become
visible \citep{hanson1992,crutchfield1997}. A practical question follows: given
a finite CA space-time, what is the simplest global periodic organization that
best explains it, and what structured residual remains once that organization
is removed? This is squarely within the ALIFE 2026 theme of living and
life-like complex adaptive systems.

Existing approaches optimize different objectives. Computational mechanics and
local causal-state methods reconstruct local statistical structure and identify
domains, particles, and interactions in detail
\citep{crutchfield1997,hanson1992,rupe2018,shalizi2006,rupe2024}. Survey- and
compression-based traditions classify rules both globally and by scale
\citep{packard1985,boccara1999,zenil2010}, and recent work systematizes CA
classification and its open problems
\citep{vispoel2022,rollier2024,hudcova2021,alfaro2024,cisneros2023}. Winnower
aims at a middle ground: a global, complexity-penalized selector for periodic
space-time templates, fast enough for wide scans while still supporting a
principled comparison between periodic explanations.

\section{The Winnower Algorithm}
The central idea is to represent each candidate period/shift pair through its
relative-periodic orbit classes and to fit a background top-down: choose the
best global periodic template, then analyze the residual mask for particles,
defects, or other coherent departures.

\paragraph{Model family and fit.}
Given a binary spacetime $U$, a shift $\bs$, and a period $p$, Winnower
considers backgrounds satisfying $B[t+p,\,\mathbf{x}+\bs \bmod \bD] = B[t,\mathbf{x}]$.
The corresponding space-time translation partitions the observations into orbit
classes $\{O_j\}$. For orbit class $O_j$ let $n_j^{(1)}$ and $n_j^{(0)}$ be the
observed numbers of ones and zeros; the Hamming-optimal background assigns each
class its majority symbol, giving a minimum residual count
$\sum_j \min\!\big(n_j^{(0)}, n_j^{(1)}\big)$. This orbit-class reduction is the
computational backbone: a seemingly global fitting problem becomes a collection
of independent binary decisions, and each candidate costs a single linear pass
over the observed sites (\cref{fig:algorithm}). For a scanned family of periods
and shifts, Winnower returns the selected period and shift, the fitted
background, the residual defect mask, and the per-candidate scores.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.99\linewidth]{algorithm_detailed_tikz.pdf}
    \caption{Winnower algorithm sketch. Each candidate period/shift pair
    induces an orbit partition, is fitted by orbit-wise majority vote, and is
    scored by Bernoulli NML. The output is a selected background together with
    a residual mask.}
    \label{fig:algorithm}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Scoring, and why a complexity penalty is necessary.}
To compare candidates, Winnower uses the Bernoulli NML score
\begin{equation}
\NML(p,\bs) \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_j n_j\,\Hb(\hat{\theta}_j)}_{\text{fit (NLL)}}
\;+\; \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_j \log_2 n_j}_{\text{complexity}},
\qquad \hat{\theta}_j = n_j^{(1)}/n_j,
\label{eq:nml}
\end{equation}
where $\Hb$ is the binary entropy and $n_j = |O_j|$; the first term is the
Bernoulli negative log-likelihood and the second is the asymptotic NML
complexity for independent Bernoulli classes \citep{shtarkov1987,grunwald2007,yamanishi2023}.
The penalty is not cosmetic. When a longer period is a velocity-matched
multiple of a shorter one, its orbit partition \emph{refines} the shorter one,
so it splits every class into finer pieces and can only lower the residual
count and the NLL --- never raise them. A selector scoring by fit alone
therefore inflates the period along every such chain, whether or not any extra
structure is present. The complexity term in \cref{eq:nml} is what neutralizes
that pressure. This boundary --- velocity-matched divisibility is exactly the
condition under which one template universally refines another --- is the
design rationale for the penalty, and its structural core is machine-checked in
Lean~4 under public continuous integration \citep{achim2025}.

\paragraph{Why selection stabilizes.}
Once candidates settle to distinct per-site NLL rates, the linear-in-horizon
fit gap between them overwhelms the logarithmic complexity penalty in
\cref{eq:nml}, so the lowest-rate candidate is eventually selected and the
choice locks. When a spacetime becomes exactly relative-periodic after a finite
transient, the smallest period is recovered, because larger multiples pay a
larger complexity penalty without improving the asymptotic fit rate.

\section{Experiments}
The empirical program asks three questions: does the selector stabilize across
dimensions, is the complexity penalty necessary, and does the residual mask
retain interesting structure once the background is removed?

\paragraph{Cross-dimensional stabilization.}
Winnower was evaluated on one-dimensional elementary CA (rules 30, 54, 110),
broad Life-like and totalistic surveys in two dimensions, and a
three-dimensional totalistic rule \citep{bays1987,bays2006}. Rule~54 locks
immediately to period~4; the tested 3D rule (semitotalistic 5,6,7,8/5,6,7,8)
locks immediately to period~1; and rules~110 and S24/B11 pass through short
transients before settling (\cref{fig:stabilization}). In every case the
winning margin \emph{grows} after the final transition. Across the detailed
case studies, every tested rule made at most two period transitions before
locking over the observed horizon range --- the finite-horizon signature of the
stabilization behavior above.

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.86\textwidth]{stabilization_summary_tikz.pdf}
    \caption{Representative stabilization patterns across one, two, and three
    dimensions. Top: selected period versus horizon. Bottom: NML margin to the
    runner-up. Both immediate locking and short-transient locking appear.}
    \label{fig:stabilization}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Why the complexity penalty matters.}
The selector ablation is sharp. Scoring the same candidate families by raw
residual count, by Bernoulli NLL, and by Bernoulli NML, only the NML selector
avoids systematic period inflation (\cref{fig:ablation}). On 105 nontrivial
Life-like rules at horizon $T{=}100$, plain NLL chooses the largest tested
period in \emph{every} case and residual minimization also favors higher
periods, whereas NML concentrates mass at period~1 (97 rules), with only a
small minority selecting period~2 (7) or period~8 (1). This is the empirical
face of the refinement argument above: once refinement guarantees monotone fit
improvement, only a complexity penalty can prevent overfitting along
divisibility chains.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.99\linewidth]{selector_ablation_tikz.pdf}
    \caption{Selector ablation on 105 nontrivial Life-like rules at $T{=}100$.
    Raw residual minimization and Bernoulli NLL are pulled toward larger
    periods; Bernoulli NML prevents that inflation by balancing fit against
    orbit-class complexity.}
    \label{fig:ablation}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Residual structure.}
The selected background is useful not only because it names a domain hypothesis
but because the residual mask preserves structured departures from it. In 1D,
an entropy-rate comparison shows that the periodic background captures the
predictable component of space-time while the residual retains substantial but
\emph{structured} uncertainty rather than pure noise (\cref{fig:entropy}),
consistent with the domains-and-particles picture of computational mechanics
\citep{crutchfield1997,rupe2024}. In 2D, rules such as S37/B11 exhibit residual
populations that persist over long horizons and scale extensively with grid
size --- exactly the localized activity that glider- and coherent-structure
detectors target \citep{secktuohmora2023,shalizi2006}.

    background captures nearly all of the predictable structure, while the
    residual mask retains substantial but structured uncertainty.}

\end{figure}

\section{Discussion}
The value of Winnower is that it provides a fast global hypothesis about domain
organization. It does not replace local causal-state reconstruction
\citep{rupe2018,rupe2024,rupe2025}, and it does not claim that the selected
period is always a ``true'' background period. Instead it provides a compact
domain hypothesis, a residual mask, and a principled way to compare periodic
explanations across large rule families. This makes it useful in two settings:
as a fast periodic-domain screen over many rules before deeper analysis, and as
a front end for follow-on structure discovery, where the residual mask marks
where particle, defect, or coherent-structure methods should focus. The same
front-end role applies to modern continuous and learned substrates whose
emergent patterns invite the same background/residual question
\citep{hamon2024,plantec2025,niklasson2021}. The tool is modest by design: a
scalable global front end, not a full semantic account of CA dynamics.

\section{Conclusion}
Winnower turns periodic-domain selection in CA space-time into a concrete
model-selection problem. Relative-periodic orbit classes make background
fitting fast, and the Bernoulli NML score explains why unpenalized selectors
overfit and why the penalized selector locks once asymptotic rate differences
emerge. Across one, two, and three dimensions the experiments reported here are
consistent with that picture. The broader claim is that global periodic-domain
selection can serve as a practical bridge between information-theoretic model
selection and the study of emergent space-time organization in artificial life.
