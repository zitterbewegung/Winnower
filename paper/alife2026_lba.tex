\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Winnower is an open-source tool that splits a cellular-automaton
spacetime into a periodic \emph{background} and a \emph{residual mask},
scanning candidate periodicities and choosing among them with a
complexity-aware score. Like every fit-based detector it faces
\emph{period inflation}: a longer-period candidate can never fit worse,
so an unpenalized selector drifts toward the largest model. We report a
result that pins down exactly when this pressure exists. For two
constant-velocity candidates, five natural conditions --- velocity-matched
divisibility, orbit-partition refinement, model-class nesting, universal
residual improvement, and universal likelihood improvement --- are all
\emph{equivalent}, and divisibility is \emph{necessary}, not merely
sufficient: whenever it fails, some spacetime makes the longer candidate
fit strictly worse. The full equivalence, including the previously open
necessity direction, is now machine-checked in Lean~4 under public
continuous integration, and an exhaustive finite-window test confirms it
over every candidate pair on a small grid. The characterization is the
precise justification for Winnower's complexity penalty, and it is
reproducible and inspectable in the tool's live web companion.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}
Cellular automata (CA) routinely produce spacetimes in which broad
periodic domains coexist with defects and particle-like activity; those
domains are the canvas against which coherent structures such as gliders
become visible \citep{hanson1992,crutchfield1997}. \emph{Winnower} is a
fast global front end for this setting: given a binary spacetime $U$ and
a grid of \emph{candidates} $(p,\bs)$ --- ``the pattern repeats every $p$
steps, drifting sideways by $\bs$'' --- it groups the cells each
candidate forces to be equal (its \emph{orbit classes}), takes a
majority vote inside each class to build the best-fitting background
$B^{*}$, and records the residual mask $M = U \oplus B^{*}$. Each
candidate costs one linear pass, so whole-catalog 1D/2D/3D surveys are
practical, and every reported number re-runs in an in-browser demo.

A selector that scores candidates by fit alone has a built-in bias.
Call $(p_2,\bs_2)$ a \emph{velocity-matched multiple} of $(p_1,\bs_1)$
when $p_2 = m\,p_1$ and $\bs_2 = m\,\bs_1 \bmod \bD$: the same drift
expressed with a longer period. Such a multiple splits every orbit class
into finer pieces, so its optimal residual count and its negative
log-likelihood ($\NLL$) can only improve. Unpenalized selection
therefore inflates the period whether or not any extra structure is
present. Winnower answers this with a complexity term --- a Bernoulli
normalized-maximum-likelihood (NML) penalty \citep{shtarkov1987,grunwald2007}
--- but a penalty is only well-motivated if we know \emph{exactly} which
comparisons carry the inflation pressure. This abstract reports that
exact boundary and its machine-checked proof.

\section{An Exact Characterization}
Fix a torus with spatial dimensions $\bD$ and temporal length $T$, and
two candidates $c_1,c_2$ acting by spacetime translation. Write
$\Pi_i$ for the orbit partition of $c_i$, $\mathcal{B}_i$ for the
model class of fields constant on $\Pi_i$-classes, $d^{*}_U(c_i)$ for
the minimum Hamming distance from $U$ to $\mathcal{B}_i$, and
$\NLL_U(c_i)$ for the orbit-class Bernoulli negative log-likelihood.

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Theorem (equivalence).} The following are equivalent:
(i)~$c_2$ is a velocity-matched multiple of $c_1$;
(ii)~$c_2$'s translation is a power of $c_1$'s;
(iii)~$\Pi_2$ refines $\Pi_1$;
(iv)~$\mathcal{B}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{B}_2$;
(v)~$d^{*}_U(c_2) \le d^{*}_U(c_1)$ for \emph{every} $U$;
(vi)~$\NLL_U(c_2) \le \NLL_U(c_1)$ for \emph{every} $U$.
\end{quote}

The forward chain (i)$\Rightarrow\cdots\Rightarrow$(vi) is routine
(cyclic-group structure; Jensen's inequality for the concavity of binary
entropy). The content is the \emph{necessity} direction: if divisibility
fails, refinement fails, and then a concrete adversarial spacetime ---
constant on $\Pi_1$-classes but mixing two of them inside one
$\Pi_2$-class --- makes $c_2$ strictly worse under both $d^{*}$ and
$\NLL$. Closing the loop requires showing that partition refinement
\emph{forces} a power (iii)$\Rightarrow$(ii): on the torus, orbits are
cosets of the cyclic subgroup a translation generates, so refinement at
the identity yields subgroup containment, and a translation is fixed by
its value at one point. Divisibility is thus the exact frontier of the
guaranteed-improvement regime: on one side longer periods always win; on
the other, the data can always punish them. This is precisely the set of
comparisons Winnower's penalty must, and does, neutralize.

\section{Machine-Checked, and Reproducible in the Tool}
We regard a mathematical claim as verified only when Lean's kernel
accepts it. The full six-way equivalence, including the necessity
direction (iii)$\Rightarrow$(ii) that earlier drafts left open, is proved
in Lean~4 against a pinned Mathlib toolchain and rebuilt on every push by
the project's public continuous-integration workflow, which additionally
greps the built artifact for placeholder tactics. Independently, an
exhaustive test enumerates all candidate pairs on a $6\times4$
space--time window and checks that partition refinement holds
\emph{iff} velocity-matched divisibility holds --- a finite-window
counterpart to the torus theorem, and a guard against silent
formalization drift.

The characterization is not a footnote to the tool; it is its
justification, and it is inspectable where the tool lives. The Winnower
web companion carries a dedicated ``Late-Breaking'' section stating the
theorem in plain terms and linking the machine-checked Lean file and its
public CI runs; and the same in-browser reproduction that backs every
other number reports, for any supported rule, seed, and horizon, the
per-period fit ($\NLL$) and penalized ($\NML$) scores side by side ---
so a visitor can watch the fit column improve monotonically along
velocity-matched chains, exactly as the theorem dictates, while the
penalized column resists.

\section{Status and Next Steps}
This is work in progress. The empirical picture (planted-ground-truth
recovery at $1.00$ joint accuracy over $642$ runs; zero false positives
on shuffled and random controls; the ablation above) is established in
the companion full paper; the new contribution here is the exact,
machine-checked characterization that explains \emph{why} the penalty is
the right shape and \emph{exactly} where it is needed. Open threads
include lifting the finite-window necessity direction (which currently
carries one nondegeneracy hypothesis, $p_2 \le T-1$) into Lean alongside
the torus form, and extending the analysis from pairwise comparisons to
the full candidate lattice the scanner actually searches. The tool, the
proofs, the tests, and the in-browser reproduction are public.
