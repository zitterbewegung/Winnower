\begin{abstract}
Cellular automata (CAs) often produce extended periodic domains separated by
localized defects, particles, and domain boundaries. We present
\emph{Winnower}, a tool for inferring a periodic domain from a finite CA
space--time diagram. For each candidate temporal period and lattice
displacement, Winnower groups observed sites into translation orbits, assigns
each orbit its majority cell state, and evaluates the resulting
template with Bernoulli normalized maximum likelihood (NML). The selected
template gives a compact domain model, while mismatched sites form a
candidate-defect mask. We report preliminary experiments on one-, two-, and
three-dimensional CAs. The selected symmetry usually settles after a short
transient, and the NML complexity term prevents the systematic period inflation
observed with Hamming residual or unpenalized likelihood alone. In a sweep of
105 nontrivial Life-like rules, NML selected period~1 for 97 rules and exposed
residual patterns that remained structured over time. These results suggest
that Winnower can provide a lightweight first pass for CA surveys, identifying
candidate domains before local particle or coherent-structure analysis. Data
and code: \url{https://github.com/zitterbewegung/Winnower}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A cellular automaton (CA) evolves a lattice by applying the same local rule to
every cell at each time step. Many CA rules generate space--time diagrams with
large repeating regions together with localized departures from those regions,
such as particles, gliders, and domain boundaries. We refer to a repeating
region as a \emph{spatiotemporal domain}: a pattern that is invariant under a
combined temporal translation and lattice displacement. Identifying this
domain provides a useful background model for studies of information transport,
coherent structures, and emergent computation in artificial life.

The practical problem is to infer such a domain from a finite observation. A
candidate should explain as much of the space--time diagram as possible without
using an unnecessarily long period. Local causal-state methods can reconstruct
detailed spatiotemporal organization \citep{rupe2024}, while CA taxonomies,
transient analyses, and compression-based approaches characterize whole rules
and global pattern complexity
\citep{vispoel2022,rollier2024,hudcova2021,alfaro2024,cisneros2023}. Here we
present Winnower as a complementary global method. It searches a finite set of
period-and-displacement candidates, returns a periodic domain template and a
residual mask, and uses a complexity penalty to avoid selecting a longer period
only because it fits the observation more closely. This late-breaking abstract
describes the method and reports preliminary results across several CA
families.

\section{Method}
Given a finite binary CA space--time block $U$, a temporal period $p$, and a
lattice-displacement vector $\bs$, Winnower fits templates satisfying
$B[t{+}p,\,\mathbf{x}{+}\bs \bmod \bD]=B[t,\mathbf{x}]$, where $\bD$ contains
the lattice dimensions. Thus, the template repeats after $p$ updates, up to the
displacement $\bs$. This translation partitions the observed sites into
translation orbits $\{O_j\}$. For each orbit, Winnower assigns the majority cell
state. The resulting template is the binary template with the smallest Hamming
disagreement for that candidate, and the mismatched sites form its domain
residual.

Winnower compares candidates with the Bernoulli NML score
%
\begin{equation}
\NML(p,\bs)=\sum_j n_j\,\Hb(\hat\theta_j)\;+\;\tfrac12\sum_j \log_2 n_j,
\label{eq:nml}
\end{equation}
%
where $n_j=|O_j|$, $n_j^{(1)}$ is the number of cells in state~1 on orbit
$O_j$, $\hat\theta_j=n_j^{(1)}/n_j$, and $\Hb$ is binary entropy. The first term
is the Bernoulli negative log-likelihood (NLL) of the orbit models. The second
term is the asymptotic NML complexity cost for fitting those models
\citep{shtarkov1987,grunwald2007,yamanishi2023}. The candidate with the lowest
combined score is selected. \Cref{fig:winnower-pipeline} summarizes the
procedure.

The complexity term addresses a recurring problem in this search. A longer
candidate moving at the same velocity can split the orbits of a shorter
candidate into smaller orbits. This added flexibility cannot worsen the Hamming
residual or NLL, so fit-only selection tends to prefer the longest available
period even when the added orbits do not represent new domain structure. NML
charges for this added flexibility. We also machine-checked the corresponding
divisibility and orbit-refinement relation in Lean~4 under public CI
\citep{achim2025}. When candidates have different NLL rates per observed cell,
the fit difference grows linearly with observation size, whereas the relevant
penalty difference grows logarithmically. This provides a simple explanation
for why the selected candidate settles as more of the space--time diagram is
observed and why the shortest equivalent candidate can be recovered when it is
included in the search.

\section{Preliminary Results}
We tested ECAs 30, 54, and 110; two-dimensional Life-like and totalistic
rules; and 3D Life B5/S45 \citep{bays1987,bays2006}.
\Cref{fig:decomp} shows the resulting decompositions for the representative
rules, and \cref{fig:stab} the corresponding horizon sweep
($T\in\{50,\dots,800\}$ in 1D/2D at seed~11; $T\in\{10,\dots,50\}$ at seed~42
in 3D, where the B5/S45 soup decays toward the quiescent domain). The selected
candidate settled immediately for ECA~54 (period~4, margin growing from 681 to
2318 bits) and 3D Life (period~1, 3324 to 7403 bits), and after a single
transition for ECA~110 ($1\!\to\!7$, the period-7 Ether domain) and S24/B11
($1\!\to\!2$). Every tested rule changed its selected candidate at most twice,
and its margin over the runner-up grew after the last change.

We also compared Hamming residual, unpenalized NLL, and NML on the Life-like
sweep. At $T{=}100$, NLL selected the largest scanned period for all 105
nontrivial rules, whereas NML did not show this systematic period inflation and
selected period~1 for 97 rules. This matches the orbit-refinement argument
above: a fit-only selector benefits from additional orbit parameters, whereas
NML trades that improvement against model complexity.

We also examined the information left in the domain residual. Entropy-rate
comparisons suggest that the inferred template captures a predictable component
of the space--time diagram while the residual retains structured variation. In
two-dimensional rules such as S37/B11, the residual population persisted and
scaled with lattice size. This behavior suggests that the mask can identify
rules worth examining with local particle, glider, or coherent-structure
detectors \citep{secktuohmora2023}.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} Every number and figure above regenerates
deterministically from the released tool: single rules via \texttt{analyze},
\texttt{analyze2d}, and \texttt{analyze3d};
\cref{fig:decomp,fig:stab} via \texttt{alife\_lba\_figures.py} after the
horizon sweep \texttt{convergence\_all\_dims.py}.

\section{Discussion and Future Work}
These preliminary results support Winnower as a lightweight first pass for
broad CA rule-space surveys. It reduces each run to a selected translation
symmetry, a periodic template, and a residual mask that can guide more detailed
local analysis.

The method assumes binary states, searches a supplied candidate set, and fits
one global periodic model. A selected template need not be unique, and residual
cells are candidate defects rather than identified particles. Winnower therefore
complements local causal-state reconstruction and related approaches
\citep{rupe2024,rupe2025}. Future work will test robustness to observation
length, boundaries, noise, and candidate-set size; connect residual masks to
local tracking; and extend Winnower to multistate, continuous, and neural CAs
\citep{hamon2024,plantec2025,niklasson2021}.
