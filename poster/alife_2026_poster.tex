\vspace{0.2em}
{\fontsize{20}{24}\selectfont Joshua Herman\hfill ALIFE 2026 poster draft\hfill Affiliation to be added\par}

\Callout{\textbf{Main message.} Fit a global periodic scaffold, score candidate periods and shifts with Bernoulli NML, and analyze the residual defect mask. The poster emphasis is 2D: broad periodic domains can coexist with persistent structured residuals that survive the fit. 1D and 3D examples act as cross-dimensional checks.}

\vspace{0.45em}
\noindent
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.29\textwidth}

\SectionHeader{Question}
Cellular automata often show broad periodic domains together with particles, interfaces, and other persistent defects. The practical problem is to decide \emph{which global period and shift best explain a finite observed spacetime}.

\Callout{This is a front end rather than a full causal-state reconstruction: find a good scaffold first, then study what remains.}

\SectionHeader{Selector}
For each candidate period $p$ and shift $\mathbf{s}$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the translation $(t,\mathbf{x}) \mapsto (t+p,\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s})$ partitions spacetime into orbit classes;
  \item majority vote on each orbit gives the nearest relative-periodic background $B^*(p,\mathbf{s})$;
  \item the residual mask is $M = U \oplus B^*(p,\mathbf{s})$;
  \item candidates are scored by Bernoulli NML rather than raw residual or raw likelihood.
\end{itemize}

\[
\mathrm{NML}(p,\mathbf{s}) =
\sum_j n_j H_b(\hat{\theta}_j)
\;+\;
\frac{1}{2}\sum_j \log_2 n_j
\]

The first term rewards pure orbit classes. The second term keeps larger templates from winning only because they have more parameters.

\SectionHeader{Why The Penalty Matters}
{\fontsize{15}{19}\selectfont
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}lX@{}}
\toprule

\midrule
Residual minimization & 16 pick $p=1$, 8 pick $p=2$, and 81 pick period $\ge 4$ \\
Bernoulli NLL & All 105 pick the maximum tested period \\

\bottomrule

}

\vspace{0.35em}
This is the cleanest poster-side argument for using NML: without complexity control, the selector systematically overfits.

For \textbf{S37/B11}, the late defect density stays close to $0.011$ across $32^2$ to $192^2$ grids, with mean late defects growing from $11.4$ to $428.5$. That is the strongest poster-side application case for persistent structured residuals.

\vspace{0.35em}
\Callout{\textbf{Poster takeaway.} The most compelling 2D story is not just ``low defect rate.'' It is that the selected scaffold captures the broad regular background while leaving behind residuals that remain structured, persistent, and scalable.}

\SectionHeader{Takeaways}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A single global selector gives usable periodic scaffolds in 1D, 2D, and 3D CA.
  \item The complexity penalty is not cosmetic. It changes the selected period qualitatively.
  \item In 2D, residual masks expose structured defects and make broad surveys feasible.
  \item The method is a front end: it does not replace computational mechanics, but it produces compact domain hypotheses worth following up with richer local analyses.
\end{itemize}
